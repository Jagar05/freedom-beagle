*The Grievance Audit holds the modern federal record up against the twenty-seven grievances of the Declaration of Independence. Quote the grievance, quote what the Founders built, lay out today's record with citations. Terms per Black's Law Dictionary. Reporting on documents, not legal advice.*

## The grievance

> "For imposing Taxes on us without our Consent"
> — Declaration of Independence, para. 19 (1776)

This is the one that started it — the grievance behind the tea in the harbor and the phrase every schoolchild can finish: no taxation without representation. The colonists did not object to taxes as such. They objected to being taxed by a legislature in which they had no vote.

## What the Founders built

Representation in the body that taxes you — for the states. And there the design has a seam:

- **U.S. Const. art. I, §§ 2–3:** the House and Senate are composed of members elected by the people **of the several States.** Representation is tied to statehood.
- **U.S. Const. art. I, § 8, cl. 17:** the seat of government — the future Washington, D.C. — is a federal district under Congress's exclusive control, not a state.
- **U.S. Const. art. IV, § 3, cl. 2:** the territories are governed by Congress under the Territory Clause, also not states.
- **U.S. Const. amend. XXIII (1961):** gave D.C. residents electors for *president* — but not one voting member of Congress.

The result is a structure in which several million citizens are taxed and governed by a Congress they cannot vote for, because they do not live in a state.

## The terms, per Black's

- **Representation** — Black's Law Dictionary (12th ed. 2024): the act of standing for another, especially the function of a legislator elected to act on constituents' behalf.
- **Territory** — per Black's, a geographical area under the jurisdiction of a nation but not admitted as a state and lacking full representation in the national legislature.
- **Suffrage** — per Black's, the right or privilege of casting a vote at a public election.

## The record today

**Washington, D.C.: roughly 700,000 citizens, no vote in Congress.** D.C. residents pay federal income tax — in fact, per capita, they pay **more federal tax than the residents of any state,** and in total more than the residents of 26 states, according to figures cited across the statehood debate. They serve in the military. And they are represented in the House by a single **non-voting** delegate and in the Senate by no one. The city's own license plates carry the protest as a slogan: **"End Taxation Without Representation."** The grievance is not an analogy in the District. It is embossed on the cars.

**The territories: roughly 3.5 million more.** Puerto Rico alone is home to about 3.2 million U.S. citizens; add Guam, the U.S. Virgin Islands, the Northern Mariana Islands, and American Samoa, and the total climbs further. Residents of the territories generally cannot vote for president, have only non-voting delegates in the House, and — depending on the territory and the tax — pay a range of federal taxes including payroll taxes that fund Social Security and Medicare. **All told, on the order of four million people** live under the American flag, owe money to the federal government, and have no voting voice in the body that levies it.

**And the reason it persists is a fact the audit must state plainly: partisan arithmetic.** Admitting D.C. as a state, or granting Puerto Rico statehood, would add members to Congress — and because the District votes heavily one way, and the territories' politics cut in contested directions, both parties calculate the Senate math before the principle. Representation for four million taxpaying citizens has been held hostage, for decades, to which party would gain by granting it. That is not a founding-era grievance. It is a living one, and both parties have their fingerprints on the status quo.

**By the numbers**

- **~700,000** — D.C. residents with no voting representation in Congress *(Census; Brennan Center)*
- **~3.5 million** — additional U.S. citizens in the territories without voting congressional representation *(Census; Puerto Rico ~3.2M)*
- **~4 million** — total Americans taxed, in various forms, without a voting member of Congress
- **More than any state** — D.C.'s per-capita federal tax burden; more in total than 26 states *(statehood-debate figures)*
- **1961** — the 23rd Amendment gave D.C. presidential electors, but never congressional votes

## The counterweight

State the other side. The Constitution ties congressional representation to statehood deliberately, and the framers created both the federal district and the territories knowing they would not be states — the seat of government was made a district precisely so no single state could hold the capital hostage. Residents of D.C. and the territories are not *disenfranchised* in the way the colonists were: they can move to a state and vote, they chose (or their forebears did) to live under territorial status, and Congress has, at various times, offered paths to statehood or altered their tax treatment (Puerto Rico residents, for instance, generally do not pay federal income tax on Puerto Rico-source income). And the remedy is available and ordinary — admission as a state, which Congress can grant by simple legislation. The grievance, defenders argue, is not that representation is *impossible* but that the political branches have not chosen to extend it.

All true. And all of it leaves the core fact standing: four million citizens pay taxes to a government they cannot vote for, the exact condition named in 1776, unresolved not because the Constitution forbids the fix but because the politics of the fix are inconvenient.

## The audit

The 1776 grievance was taxation without representation. The 2026 record is roughly four million Americans — in the capital of the country founded on that phrase, and in its territories — taxed by a Congress in which they have no vote, a condition sustained less by constitutional necessity than by partisan advantage. The tea is long out of the harbor. The principle it was thrown over is still, for millions, unhonored.

*Quod omnes tangit ab omnibus approbari debet* — what touches all must be approved by all. It was old law when the colonists invoked it. It is older now, and still waiting.
